02 — The Story Inside

What the internet can't find about Demera.

Tigist Reda — born in Tigray, the seventh of eleven children, grew up cooking for a household of twenty. No culinary school. The recipes passed hand to hand. She came to Chicago in 1997, found an empty corner at Broadway and Lawrence, and opened the doors in November 2007. That was almost 18 years ago.

The food is Tigrayan — not generic Ethiopian. Injera fermented for days. Berbere ground from her mother's ratio. Tej made in-house, which most Chicago Ethiopian restaurants don't do. A plant-based menu rooted in centuries of Ethiopian Orthodox fasting tradition — 180 days a year without animal products, for over a thousand years.

When civil war broke out in Tigray in 2020, Tigist couldn't contact her family for nearly two years. Her response: organize Chicago Chefs Cook for Tigray — raising money for a wellness center for women and children in a Sudanese refugee camp. Now she's building a $6-9 million mixed-use development in Bronzeville — a 7,000 sq ft restaurant, rooftop dining, commissary kitchen, and 14-16 residential units including affordable housing.

Michelin Recommended. Eater Chicago 38. Chicago Magazine 50 Best. The Infatuation. Resy. Check Please! WTTW. Time Out Market Chicago. 17+ years in Uptown.

The Point

This story — the origin, the recipes, the fundraisers, the Bronzeville expansion — lives in press articles and word of mouth. It does not live on the feed. That's the gap we close.

03 — The Communal Table

Every great brand has one thing.

Not a tagline. The thing people feel when they experience you — the invisible thread that runs through everything. For Demera, that thread is the communal table. We're not inventing it. We're naming it.

The injera — one platter, shared by everyone. The food format itself demands togetherness.
The gursha — you feed someone else with your hands. There is no more intimate act at a dining table.
The coffee ceremony — three rounds. You're supposed to stay.
The fasting tradition — 180 days a year, the table adapts. The gathering doesn't stop.
Tigist's childhood — cooking for 20+ family members. The communal table is where she learned everything.
The fundraisers — gathering Chicago chefs around a table to raise money for refugees.
Bronzeville — building a bigger table. Literally.
50% first-timers — every night, half the room is being welcomed to the table for the first time.

04 — The System

Three lanes, two formats, one rule.

Every piece of content falls into one of three lanes. All three lead to the same place: the communal table.

Educational

Teach people something they didn't know. 50% of the dining room is first-timers — they're curious but maybe intimidated. Educational content is the onramp.

"Why Ethiopians don't use forks" · "Ethiopia invented coffee" · "What is gursha?" · "180 days without meat"

Story-Driven

Tell the human stories behind the food. People don't share food photos — they share stories that make them feel something. Tigist's story is invisible online.

"I'm the seventh of eleven children" · "This corner sat empty for two years" · "We're building a restaurant in Bronzeville"

Process

Show how the food is made. You can't taste through a screen — but you can hear berbere hit kibbe butter, watch injera bubbles form, see stews being arranged on the platter. Process content makes people hungry. Hungry people book tables.

The Structure of Every Video

Sound → Story → Table.

The hook is sensory (first 1-3 seconds). The middle is meaning. The resolve is always the table — hands on injera, people together, the communal moment.

Two core formats.

Repeatable systems, not one-off ideas. Each format can be produced week after week.

Format 01

The Mitad Reel

15-second video of injera being made. Batter poured in a spiral, bubbles forming, steam rising, peeled in one motion.

Why it works: Transformation arc — viewers watch something formless become something finished. Same mechanic as viral brick-oven pizza content, but nobody's posting injera. Novel + satisfying = shares. Cadence: 2x/week.

Format 02

The Cultural Story

30-60 second video. Someone on camera telling one specific story about the food, the tradition, or the restaurant. Not a recipe — a meaning.

Why it works: Highest-ceiling format. Nobody can copy your story. When an owner speaks about what the food means — not how it's made, but why it matters — that content breaks through everything. Cadence: 1-2x/week.
