02 — The Story Inside

What the internet can't find about Demera.

Tigist Reda — born in Tigray, the seventh of eleven children, grew up cooking for a household of twenty. No culinary school. The recipes passed hand to hand. She came to Chicago in 1997, found an empty corner at Broadway and Lawrence, and opened the doors in November 2007.

The food is Tigrayan — not generic Ethiopian. Injera fermented for days. Berbere ground from her mother's ratio. Tej made in-house. A plant-based menu rooted in centuries of Ethiopian Orthodox fasting tradition — 180 days a year without animal products.

When civil war broke out in Tigray in 2020, Tigist organized Chicago Chefs Cook for Tigray. Now she's building a $6-9M mixed-use development in Bronzeville — 7,000 sq ft restaurant, rooftop dining, commissary kitchen, 14-16 residential units including affordable housing.

Michelin Recommended · Eater Chicago 38 · Chicago Magazine 50 Best · The Infatuation · Resy · Check Please! WTTW · Time Out Market Chicago

The Point

This story lives in press articles and word of mouth. It does not live on the feed. That's the gap — and that's exactly what a positioning anchor solves.

03 — The Positioning Anchor

The Communal Table.

This is the one thing everything builds on. Not a tagline — the positioning anchor that every piece of content, every format, every caption filters through.

Every great brand has one thing people feel when they experience it. For Demera, it's the communal table. We're not inventing it — we're naming what's already true. It shows up everywhere:

The injera — one platter, shared by everyone. The food format itself demands togetherness.
The gursha — you feed someone else with your hands. There is no more intimate act at a dining table.
The coffee ceremony — three rounds. You're supposed to stay.
The fasting tradition — 180 days a year, the table adapts. The gathering doesn't stop.
Tigist's childhood — cooking for 20+ family members. The communal table is where she learned everything.
The fundraisers — gathering Chicago chefs around a table to raise money for refugees.
Bronzeville — building a bigger table. Literally.
50% first-timers — every night, half the room is being welcomed to the table for the first time.

How the Anchor Works

Every piece of content passes through one filter: does this bring people to the table? If yes — post it. If no — it doesn't belong. No random posts. No filler. Everything compounds on a single idea until Demera owns it.

The Rule

Sound → Story → Table. Every video opens with something sensory (first 1-3 seconds). The middle is meaning. The resolve is always the table — hands on injera, people together, the communal moment. Same structure, infinite variations.

Format 01 — The Mitad Reel

15 seconds. Injera being made.

Batter poured in a spiral. Bubbles forming. Steam rising. Peeled in one motion. Transformation arc — same mechanic as viral brick-oven pizza content, but nobody's posting injera.

Anchor connection: The injera IS the table. It's the shared surface. Every Mitad Reel ends at the table. Cadence: 2x/week.

Format 02 — The Cultural Story

30-60 seconds. One story about the food, the tradition, or the restaurant.

Someone on camera telling one specific thing. Not a recipe — a meaning. Nobody can copy your story.

Anchor connection: Every story resolves at the table — the gursha, the coffee ceremony, Tigist cooking for twenty. Cadence: 1-2x/week.
